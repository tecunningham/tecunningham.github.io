You Know When You’re Wrong

Author

Tom Cunningham

Published

February 25, 2025

Borel said you don’t know when you’re wrong.

Borel said:

“the inner certainty of someone who is making a mistake and answering wrongly is exactly the same as the inner certainty of someone who knows and is not making a mistake.”1

But you do know when you’re wrong.

I think Borel stumbles here. The two types of inner certainty are not the same, they are different. When you discover your mistake, when you see the truth, you recognize her, she is not a stranger.

I am going to give a sermon on mistakes.
But further below this there’s a seminar version, with definitions and claims.
Some recent mistakes I made:
  • I under-estimated the share of users who performed a certain action over a 28-day period.
  • I dumped the milk straight into the flour, making the waffles lumpy.
  • I bought a pair of shoes that are ugly.
  • I said something tart to someone I love.
  • I kicked the ball too hard.
  • I didn’t mention an exception when making a generalization in a paper.
  • I drew a pair of eyes too almond-like, they didn’t fit the nose.
  • I picked up my phone to check if I had new messages.
  • I bought an expensive croissant to take home to my family.
  • I thought “cuando” meant while.
When I made these mistakes I immediately recognized them as mistakes.
I see the mistake and I get a shock of recognition. I open the cupboard to put away the can of coconut milk I bought and I see a dozen cans of coconut milk looking back at me. The mistake sometimes triggers a memory of making the same mistake my whole life, and it sometimes triggers a vision of making the same mistake through my remaining decades.
People who have studied a little mathematics will often give lectures.
They will say that you didn’t make a mistake, that’s ex post & this is ex ante. They will say attempted murder should be a crime, murder should not.
That distinction picks out a certain type of mistake but I don’t think it’s the most common type of mistake. They are talking about mistakes from lack of information but we rarely lack information, we much more commonly lack clarity.
Imagine Borel in his study in Paris in 1941.
He has been granted a vision into the crystalline structure of probability and he feels he can use this grace to set in order the many disordered ideas people have about judgment. But he overreaches. His satisfaction with the theory of probability gave him a hubris, an inner certainty that is qualitatively different from the inner certainty of people who are right.
When Aristotle is reincarnated he will not say his problem was lack of information.
Aristotle will visit the library and see that all the pieces of the jigsaw have been rearranged: the dolphin is now next to the cow, not the fish. The far East is next to the far West. The evening star is next to the morning star, and the sun fits in among one set of stars, and the moon a different set of stars. The races and sexes of humans are closer to each other than he had thought, and they also nestle close to the apes and the monkeys.
He will see that we have collected more jigsaw pieces than he had but he will also observe that our arrangement of the pieces makes better sense of his own smaller collection, and he will kick himself for his mistake. His problem was not a lack of information, but a lack of clarity.

Seminar Version

You have truth, signal, and decision.
Let the truth be \(v\), the signal \(x\), and the decision \(\hat{v}\).
An ex post mistake is choosing the wrong thing: \[\hat{v}\neq v.\]
An ex ante mistake is choosing the wrong thing given your information: \[\hat{v}\neq E[v|x].\]
My claims.
  1. When we talk about mistakes we are almost always thinking of ex ante mistakes: mistakes due to lack of clarity, mistakes we could have anticipated. My list of mistakes above are all in this category. Decision theorists will ask, why say someone has the information \(x\) if it is not revealed in their decisions? I say that the decisions are inconsistent, and those inconsistencies reveal the fragmented nature of information aggregation (paper 1, paper 2).
  1. Humans decision-making is constrained more by a lack of thought than a lack of information. If you had marinated longer you could have eventually achieved enlightenment.

Appendix

A gratuitous mistake in cutting scones.
I was cutting a roll of dough into squares to make scones, and as I let the knife fall I felt that I’d made a mistake but I wasn’t sure what it was.
It was a mistake of indecision: I had been hesitating on whether there was enough dough left for 3 strips or for 2: should I aim to cut a half or a third? Instead I cut down between them, a gratuitous indecisiveness, and I got one set of scones too-thin and another too-thick. Like a signwriter whose letters become progressively bunched up towards the end of the sign, a slow motion traffic accident.
Many of my mistakes are like this: trying to satisfy two people I love and satisfying neither; avoidance of awkwardness leading to even greater awkwardness.
Émile Borel (1950) Probability and Certainty

“The problem of error has preoccupied philosophers since earliest antiquity. According to the subtle remark made by a famous Greek philosopher, the man who makes a mistake is twice ignorant, for he does not know the correct answer, and he does not know that he does not know it. This second ignorance is the really serious one, for the inner certainty of someone who is making a mistake and answering wrongly is exactly the same as the inner certainty of someone who knows and is not making a mistake. We all know that we are subject to error and that we sometimes make mistakes, entirely in good faith.”

Neil Postman says our problems are not due to a lack of information.

Talking in 1984 about the age of computers:

“Is the war in Bosnia a result of a lack of information? If children are dying of starvation in Ethiopia, does it occur because of a lack of information? does racism in South Africa exist because of a lack of information?

“If criminals roam the streets of New York and Los angeles, do they do so because of a lack of information? Or let’s come down to a more personal level. If you and your spouse are unhappy together and end your marriage in divorce, will it happen because of a lack of information?

“If your children misbehave and bring shame to your family, does it happen because of a lack of information? If someone in your family has a mental breakdown, will it happen because of a lack of information? Well, I think you’ll have to concede that what ails us, what causes us the most misery and pain, at both cultural and personal levels, has nothing to do with the sort of information made accessible by computers.”
