Forecasts of AI & Economic Growth

Author

Tom Cunningham

Published

October 25, 2025

I’ve collected forecasts of AI’s effect on growth over 2025-2035.
I’m mixing together forecasts of GDP, TFP, and labor productivity; I’m mixing forecasts for the US, EU, and World. Some forecasts aren’t explicitly over a 10 year horizon but I hope I’m not being unfair. Please send me an email if I’m misinterpreting one of these.
Economists say 0.1–1.5%.
The two exceptions are Baily, Brynjolfsson, and Korinek (2023) and Korinek and Suh (2024).
AI people say 3–30%.

Forecasts by people involved in AI are between 3% and 30%, with the exception of Andrej Karpathy, who says he expects GDP growth to remain on historical trends.

There are also some forecasts by AI-involved people which do not mention GDP because they expect dramatic things to happen, e.g. human extinction or post-scarcity, such that GDP is no longer relevant.

Forecasters expect modest impacts.

Forecasting markets appear to expect relatively flat economic variables over the next 10 years. As of October 2025, Metaculus shows expectations over the next century:

  • World productivity growth increases smoothly from 3% to 5%.
  • Labor force participation falls smoothly from 84% to 36%

The changes are large but it takes 100 years, and the transition is relatively smooth. The smoothness is consistent with either (1) shared expectations of smooth growth; (2) shared expectations of discontinuous changes but uncertainty about when; (3) shared expectations of discontinuous growth but disagreement about when (i.e. disagreement between forecasters).

Financial markets expect modest impacts.
Asset prices do not seem to anticipate dramatic effects from an intelligence explosion, though they are hard to interpret. Chow, Halperin, and Mazlish (2024) argues that if the markets expect AGI then real interest rates should go very high, for two reasons: (1) if people expect dramatically higher incomes today they will save less and spend more (pushing interest rates up); (2) the returns on AI investments will be very high (pushing interest rates up).
It seems odd to expect high capabilities growth but small economic growth.

Tyler Cowen & Andrej Karpathy both predict that AGI is imminent, but also that economic growth will not move by more than 0.5%/year. They give a variety of reasons for expecting limited growth effects, some are static (low substitutability), some are dynamic (slow diffusion, complementary innovation). However I but I would love to understand the quantitative importance of these factors.

Suppose it takes 20 years to fully diffuse, and each year we experience 0.5% incremental growth, then the equilibrium impact of human-level intelligence will be a 10% increase in output. It’s hard for me to imagine human-level AI would only increase output by 10% in equilibrium, when we’ve already increased output/capita by more than 1000% over the last 100 years. A prediction that an effectively unlimited supply of cognitive labor (i.e. AGI) would cause an 0.5% annual growth seems to imply either extraordinarily low substitutability or extraordinarily slow diffusion.

Many economic forecasts assume AI will stop getting better.

It is worth emphasizing that many forecasts of AI’s impact assume that AI is a single technology which will slowly diffuse through the economy, i.e. they assume that (1) AI will not get any better than it is today, and (2) AI will not help with productivity growth, e.g. through improving R&D. I believe this is true of both Acemoglu (2024) and Aghion and Bunel (2024) (Baily, Brynjolfsson, and Korinek (2023) includes both level and growth effects).

I don’t feel either paper puts in sufficient work to justify these assumptions. The capabilities of AI have been growing tremendously over the past few years, why should we assume that this growth will stop?

The impact in 2024 was perhaps ≈0.5%.

We can estimate in a few ways:

  • Bick, Blandin, and Deming (2024) estimate 2% of working hours are on chatbots, and with a variety of assumptions about time saved they get aggregate productivity of 0.1-0.9%.
  • Collis and Brynjolfsson (2025) ask chatbot users what they would need to be paid to forego using the app for a month, and the average answer is $100. Multiplying this out they estimate the total value of chatbots is $98B, which is 0.3% of a $30T US GDP.
  • Humlum and Vestergaard (2025) finds chatbot users self-report approximately 3% productivity improvement, but there’s no detectable impact on wages or employment.
AI may increase welfare without increasing GDP.

AI is already providing a great deal of value (there are 700M ChatGPT users), but the value mostly won’t show up in GDP by our normal accounting methods. In fact it’s plausible that AI reduces GDP because it reduces demand for expertise: I no longer call my garage-door-repair guy, because ChatGPT tells me how to fix the door. Services are generally accounted for in GDP just by the wages paid to service-providers. If people substitute from human service-providers towards AI then measured GDP will fall even though true output has increased. If service-providing firms pass through their cost-savings to customers then their measured contribution to GDP will fall. The same argument applies to much older technologies – the printing press, the encylopedia, YouTube. I don’t believe any of the papers below consider this effect.

May 2023 Baily, Brynjolfsson, and Korinek (2023) +2.8% “The projection labeled “Level” assumes that generative AI raises the level of productivity and output by an additional 18% over ten years, as suggested by the illustrative numbers we discussed for the first channel. After ten years, growth reverts to the baseline rate. The third projection labeled “Level+Growth” additionally includes a one percentage point boost in the rate of growth over the baseline rate, resulting from the additional innovation triggered by generative AI.”
June 2023 McKinsey Global Institute (2023) +0.1-0.6% “genAI alone: +0.1-0.6 pp/yr labor productivity through ~2040; combined with other tech/automation: +0.5-3.4 pp/yr.”

This doesn’t pin down the incremental effect of AI on growth, but it’s presumably between 0% and 2%.
