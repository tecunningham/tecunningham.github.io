The Workshop

The premise:

“Is the dignity that work confers inseparable from the economic exchange currently at the heart of wage labor, or can it be grounded in other forms of contribution, service, and human relationship?

Two scenarios:

  1. Scenario 1: The Post-Work Society. (“’what happens from a theological/faith perspective once wage labor disappears?”)

    “AI replaces almost all of labor while maximizing leisure time and quality of life. There are effective coordinated global redistribution efforts ensuring an adequate standard of living for everyone.

  2. Scenario 2: The Great Pushback (“what if wage labor still exists, but we are subordinate to the machines?”)

    “Societies make the choice to preserve human jobs through professional licensing, human-in-the-loop requirements or job guarantee schemes. This shapes the development of AI as an increasingly powerful tool that augments workers.

Observations

We are discussing what would happen if the elevator stalls.

The scenarios seem .

The scenarios were about worlds where work was mostly the same, but robots did it instead of humans. This doesn’t seem very far off given our current state of technology, & in fact I think of this as the elevator stopping.

If the elevator does keep going up, then the scenarios we are discussing would only be a transition phase.

If we build truly superhuman intelligence, what are the implications? The best analogy I can think of is a visitor from a future civilization, like a bronze-age civilization suddenly encountering a galleon from the machine-age. In this case we may have to worry about more things than the dignity of work.

A concrete vision: idling in apartment buildings.

We have some historical precedents for living post-scarcity.
  • UAE / rentier state.
    • Classically rentier states are less accountable to the population.
    • Kuwait: 80% of natives are in public sector.
  • Religious minorities.
    • Israeli Haredi have 50% labor-force partricipation, intead of 85%.
    • Monks in Tibet & Mongolia were historically as high as 20-40% of males.
  • Long-term unemployed.
    • 20% of young American men w/o education are outside the labor force, many w disability benefits.
    • Period of high long-term unemployment rates in Europe.
  • Social dividend.
    • Alaska pays $1-2K/year.
    • Norway uses the money to fund social services.
  • Aristocrats / leisure class.
  • Hunter-gatherers.
    • Sahlins (1966) “The Original Affluent Society”, said they only worked 3-4 hours a day, though later debunked.
  • Retirees. Most people used to work roughly until they died, now they typically have 20 years of retirement (median retirement at 62, at which point you have 20 years life expectancy)
We can choose our values.

  1. Nietzsche, 1880s, “revaluation of values”

  2. Lippman (1922) “Public Opinion”.

  3. Keynes (1930) “Economic Possibilities for our Grandchildren” – He said the emphasis on the virtue of hard work is old fashioned & we need to leave it behind, those virtues were specific to a certain period, & we’ll have to learn to rise above them.

  4. Bertrand Russell (1932) “In Praise of Idleness”

    • “Like most of my generation, I was brought up on the saying “Satan finds some mischief still for idle hands to do.”

    • “a great deal of harm is being done in the modern world by the belief in the virtuousness of work, and that the road to happiness and prosperity lies in an organized diminution of work.

    • “This is the morality of the Slave State, applied in circumstances totally unlike those in which it arose.

  5. Discourse in the 1930s was much more about active choice of values: Fascism, existentialism, propaganda. Post-war values we drew a cover over it.

  6. Skinner (1971) “Beyond Freedom & Dignity”

    • No such thing as free will or dignity
    • Social engineering

    “A scientific analysis of behavior must assume that a person’s behavior is controlled by his genetic and environmental histories rather than by the person himself as an initiating agent.”

    “A person who has been controlled by positive reinforcement is said to deserve credit; the control is not recognized.”

    “The problem is to arrange contingencies of reinforcement under which people behave in ways that will produce a better culture.”

  7. 18th century turn to capitalism

    • Hirschman, “The Passions and the Interests”
    • 18th century discourse: it’s good to encourage people to follow their material interests, healthy diversion from status competition.

Awkwardness of our political philosophy.

We don’t have a common political philosophy so these things are all hard to think about. As Alasdair Macintyre says: our principles are a mixtures of welfare-maximization, rights, virtues, traditions.

Akwardness of pluralism: we say each person should pursue what they want, yet also:

  1. We understand that those values are determined by circumstances, so odd to think of them as sacrosanct;
  2. People don’t think of their values as just like their favorite color, they think of them as having meaning outside themselves;
  3. We have strong opinions about the values of our children.
