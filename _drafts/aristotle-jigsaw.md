I feel like Aristotle would recognize a lot of the pieces in the jigsaw that we're working on, but he would be surprised at where they've ended up.

The dolphin is next to the dog, not the fish. The far East is next to the far West. The evening star is next to the morning star, and the sun fits in among one set of stars, and the moon a different set of stars. The different races and sexes of humans probably are closer to one another than he would've expected, and humans much close to the rest of the animals.

I think you could just show the jigsaw to Aristotle, how we've rearranged all the pieces that he already had available, and he'd recognize it as basically right because of the beautiful symmetric patterns that appear. It's true that we've accumulated a lot of new pieces that he didn't have, but the patterns that those new pieces helped to form are, in retrospect, visible in the pieces he already had.

There are also collections of pieces which Aristotle was working on fitting together but which we have given up on. Early philosophers tried to slot together the different types of virtue, the different types of republic, the different types of human relationship, supposing that there is some fundamental pattern which makes sense of them each -- the four humours, the seven vices. That expectation is much weaker today.
