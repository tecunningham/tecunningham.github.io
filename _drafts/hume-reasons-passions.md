Hume said “reason is, & ought to be, only the slave of the passions.”

This was the opposite of the usual way that people would describe the relationship between reason and passions: normally reason controls the passions, or there is a struggle between reason and passions. 

The standard interpretation is that he's pointing out an error of logic: that a common mistake is for people to call *desires* rational or irrational -- e.g. to say that the desire for fame is irrational -- whereas it is actually only *choices* that can properly be called such, because rationality means contributing to some higher desire.

However there's a different way of putting his point, which makes it more a question of fact than a question of logic: as a disagreement over whether or not certain desires are fundamental or only instrumental. Is your desire for cheese for cheese *qua* cheese, or only insofar as cheese contributes to some higher goal? If your desire for cheese is instrumental, then we can call that desire irrational if it fails to contribute to that higher desire.

From this new angle Hume's point is more empirical. Philosophers prior to Hume had tended to suppose that most desires (lust, sympathy, sloth) all descended angel-like from some higher fundamental desire, though it was obviously obscure and arguable exactly what that was, and precisely how the apparent conflicts between lower desires could be resolved.

But Hume was he thought that a lot of these desires just existed independently and in competition, without being fundamentally connected, i.e., they are themselves fundamental desires, not instrumental, and so not capable of being called rational or otherwise. 

For a very long time philosophers had been searching for the unifying principle among the diverse desires. By the time Hume arrived (1711) it was getting to look a bit silly, & Hume announced it was time to call off the search.
