Hume said “reason is, & ought to be, only the slave of the passions.”

This was a provocative thing to say in the 18th century - at the time people would say that reason restrains the passions, or that there is a struggle between reason and passions, not that passions are the master of reason.

The standard interpretation of Hume is that he's pointing out an error of logic: he's saying that it's a mistake is for people to call *desires* reasonable or unreasonable -- e.g. to say that the desire for fame is unreasonable -- because in fact only *choices* that can properly be called reasonable, as reasonableness can only be judged with respect to some goal, i.e. a choice is rational insofar as it furthers some goal.

However there's a different way of putting his point, which makes it more a question of fact than a question of logic: as a disagreement over whether or not certain desires are fundamental or only instrumental.

Is your desire for cheese a desire for cheese *qua* cheese, or only insofar as cheese contributes to some higher goal? If your desire for cheese is instrumental, then we can call that *desire* irrational, depending on whether it contribute to the higher desires.

Put this way Hume's point is more empirical. Philosophers prior to Hume had tended to suppose that most desires -- lust, sympathy, sloth -- all descended angel-like from some higher fundamental desire, though, they would say, it remained obscure and arguable exactly what that fundamental purpose is, and precisely how the apparent conflicts between lower desires could be resolved.

So Hume instead thought that a lot of these desires just existed independently and in competition, without being fundamentally connected. These passions are themselves fundamental, not instrumental, and so not capable of being called rational or irrational. Thus reason is the slave of the passions.

Put another way: for a very long time philosophers had been searching for the unifying principle among the diverse desires. By the time Hume arrived (1711) it was getting to look hopeless, & Hume announced it was time to call off the search. The jigsaw does not have a solution, the pieces just don't fit together.
