## (2) A Picture is Two Things at Once

A good drawing is trying to be two things at once:

1. It's recognizable as the thing it's meant to be a picture of.
2. The lines themselves make a pretty pattern.

It's hard to define what makes a pretty pattern but some things are: symmetry, similarity between different parts, separation of different objects (so they don't overlap), consistency in the width of lines, consistency in the spaces between lines.

## What's the difference between naive art and amateur art?

**Naive art:** Mexican grocery-store painting & votive paintings, Rousseau, Ramirez. Drawings by young children.
**Amateur art:** Drawings from a life-drawing class.
   
What's the difference? Amateur art often has something a bit shameful about it. Unpleasant to look at, and the people who make it know that, they're embarrassed to share it. The majority of my own drawings I would put in this category: they are *bad*, I am embarrassed by them, prefer people don't see them.

**(1) Attempting something beyond your ability to pull it off.** Like a musician attempting a piece that's too hard for them: the tempo slips and they miss some notes. Would've been better if they'd tried something less ambitious. Yet somehow this is a common weakness: there's some contagious self-deception that causes people to reach beyond their skills and make ugly art when they could've been less ambitious and made something simple but nice. There must be some voice in their head egging them on, telling them to be more ambitious, even though it's self-defeating.
   
**(2) Trying to copy nature instead of paralleling it.** A good drawing is something which satisfies two things simultaneously: (1) it evokes some scene in the world, and (2) it makes a nice pattern on the paper. Perhaps amateur art neglects the second part: it tries to faithfully render what it sees, and the pattern on the paper end up just a mess, no harmony.
      
Gombrich says that art is best thought-of as a collection of techniques: painters build up a library of tricks: how to paint eyes, how to paint velvet, how to paint mountains, which they adapt to the situation. They learn tricks that are harmonious with the rest of their style. Progress in art isn't from seeing things more accurately, but from adding new techniques. His slogans: “making before matching”, “schema and correction”. 

This is perhaps just a *mistake*, not necessarily something to be ashamed of.

**Some characteristics of amateur art:**
   
- It never gets finished to satisfaction. People are embarrassed about sharing it because it's stuck in contradiction that you cannot resolve.

- Using a pencil very lightly across the page: not willing to make a firm decision, everything's tentative.

- Use foreshortening instead of just drawing everybody the same size. But foreshortening is hard to do right, so it ends up ugly.

- Trying to draw what you see instead of starting from a pattern on the paper, and adapting that pattern to resemble what you see. (Exceptions to this: some schools teach techniques to do very photorealistic copying, and this is perhaps a shortcut through the dead zone of amateur art).

- Lack of harmony because different parts are copied from different parts of the scene, and no thought to the logical problem of getting them to fit together on the page.

- Starting with a detail instead of starting with an outline. You start drawing the eye, without first sketching a big oval for the face. Very difficult to recover from this. In some sense it's obvious you *should* do the outline first, but there's some weird resistance. Perhaps because the outline is hard: the proportions probably will be wrong, and that'll doom the rest of the picture. Doing just the eye, you're less likely to mess it up. But of course the final picture will be distorted even more if you don't do this. So it's some scaredy-cat delaying of the inevitable. (Qualifications: (a) if you're drawing on top of a scheme that can harm the quality of the lines, making them less free; (b) if you've practiced drawing without a schema you eventually get good at it.)

**Q: if you face these problems can you suddenly avoid making amateur art?**

      
   - **aesthetic failure and moral failure.** If someone draws something ugly their moral sense is probably ugly.
   
      - (1) self-deception in attempting something harder than you have the skill for.
      - (2) self-deception in thinking your picture looks adequate, even though you would recognize it as bad if someone else did it.

      - (5) mistake of trying to copy nature, instead of paralleling it. Not sure if this can be connected to a moral failing, apart from point (1), that it comes from trying to do something you can't do.

## 2020-05-15 Friday ---------------- types of aesthetic argument

A good reddit post on whether cheeseburgers ought to be really tall. The comments cover every type of aesthetic argument:

1. **Difference in tastes** -- argument that you can't argue over tastes -- some people like them, some people don't. That's that.
2. **Functional** -- arguments about how much time and effort it takes to eat, how each bit should have a combination of ingredients, about the effect of patty-thickness on internal cooking.
3. **Equilibrium** -- argument that the restaurants who make these burgers are not making a mistake: they are just responding to consumer demand.
4. **Teleology** -- arguing over the definition of a burger, what a burger *is*. These arguments seem illogical but also weirdly compelling.
  - "The design of a burger, as I understand it, is essentially getting a full meal in condensed form." 
  - "The point of a burger is to have all these tasty complimentary items in one bite." 
  - "Sandwiches and burgers are meant to be eaten a certain way. Hand to mouth. If the design of your food is incapable of serving the purpose it is meant to, it is a failed product."
5. **Social significance.** -- arguments that (a) people like tall burgers because they signify luxury, excess, premium; or (b) people dislike tall burgers because they signify middle-brow tastes.
6. **Anti-functionalism** -- the impracticality of tall burgers is a feature: we like them because of their impracticality, not in spite of it.

## Aesthetic Evaluation

1. **Starting point: tastes are a weighted average of objective and subjective.** Think of the space of all possible burgers, i.e. all different combinations of ingredients, and your preferences over all these burgers. We can break your preferences down into two parts:
      1. Objective / functional preferences.
      2. Subjective / taste-based preferences.
   
   Objective preferences can be legitimately argued over, but subjective cannot. E.g., the ratio of baking-powder to flour in the dough for the bun has some objective optimum, it is an instrumental argument: what's the best means to achieve some ends. But the choice between medium-rare and well-done does not, it is an end in itself.

2. **Wrinkle: describing subjective preferences as objective.**
   - What makes this more interesting is that people often describe subjective preferences as if they are objective preferences. This manifests in a few ways: (a) inventing reasons to prefer something that they like for another reason; (b) preferences shifting with context, to satisfy objective equivalences.

3. **Wrinkle: people who say they have functional tastes often have tastes for the *appearance* of functionality.**
   - I generally like things without decoration, I like work clothes, I like kitchen equipment made for restaurants. But in fact part of this is a preference for things that *look* functional: e.g. I wouldn't want to wear a t-shirt if it had a prominent logo, even if the functionality was the same; I would hesitate getting a kitchen appliance if it looked ornamental & insubstantial, even if it actually worked well.

4. **Q: are your subjective tastes good or bad?**
    - It's hard to judge peoples' subjective tastes, but we can judge their *malleability*: when people *think* their tastes are determined by one set of features, but they're actually determined by some other set. E.g., (1) people who say they prefer medium-rare steak because of the flavor, but it's actually because they saw a character on a TV show who talked about medium-rare steak; (2) .

5. **Criticism as performance.**
   - Many people spend a lot of time complaining about things they don't like -- and that complaining serves a function to show their own discernment.
   - There is a list of common things to complain about: Guy Fieri, Nickelback, McMansions, Kenny G, cargo shorts, video games, Marvel movies, autotuning, starbucks, tall burgers.
   - A common theme is to complain about things that are attempts to be high-class, but fall short: people complain about starbucks coffee, not donut-shop coffee; complain about tall burgers, not mcdonalds burgers; complain about Norman Rockwell, not Walt Disney; complain about McMansions, not row housing; complain about Kenny G, not C&C Music Factory.

6. **Application to short-burgers vs tall-burgers.**
   1. From the baseline weighted-average model, we might say that different people have different subjective tastes, but there's room for argument over objective features -- e.g., people could be mistaken about .
   2. More deeply, two reasons why tall-burger people might be wrong:
      1. Tall burgers mistakenly think more-is-better.
      2. Tall burgers put too-much weight on appearance.
   3. But most of the complaining is probably performative -- that aspirational burgers are low-class.

7. **Smaller points.**
   - **Arguing about aesthetics: conditioning on others' disagreement.** If you're in a disagreement with another person you have to condition on the fact that they disagree. if you believe X, but other person believes not-X, then should have some theory of why they are wrong, how they made a mistake.

## Bad Art Exposes Ugly Things

**Bad art is actively bad.** Bad art is not the same as not-very-good art. Bad art is like a bad smell, bad breath, like a hug from someone you don't want to hug, it's something that you don't want to be in the same room as. It's not the same as foreign art: from a different place or different time and which you can't judge it, you don't know wether it's good or bad. Bad art is transparent, so you can see through it & see motives wriggling around -- needy, selfish, sentimental, jealous, mawkish. You see wish fulfillment - it's not made by someone who became an artist in order to make art, it is someone who makes art so they can be an artist.

## Painting & Iteration

**Painting & iteration.** We can recognize when a picture looks good or bad but at the same time can't say what would make it better or worse. The knowledge is implicit. This means that once you start painting you quickly see that there's something wrong with it, and when you try to fix it, it looks wrong in a different way, and you're in a loop.

The loop can end a few different ways: (1) you accept some set of flaws and say it's finished; (2) you keep circling, adding more brushstrokes, never admit defeat and never finish; (3) you eventually deceive yourself into thinking that the flaws are gone -- and into thinking that other people are mistaken, or jealous, when they seem to perceive flaws.

Given this problem, that it's hard to anticipate whether something will look right, it makes sense to just produce a lot and throw out the ones that don't work. People who throw out very little work may be people for whom the self-deception has hardened.

It must be possible, eventually, to figure out how to smooth out all the bugs in a picture but it hardly seems realistic in a single lifetime -- perhaps if you stick to a very stylized set of techniques. Painters have been working for thousands of years and are still learning new things about representation.

Tolstory, in *Anna Karenina*: "Vronsky’s interest in painting and the Middle Ages did not last long. He had enough taste for painting to be unable to finish his picture. The picture came to a standstill. He was vaguely aware that its defects, inconspicuous at first, would be glaring if he were to go on with it."

Quentin Blake says somewhere in his autobiography that he would often have to throw out his best-looking drawings because they wouldn't be consistent with the style of the rest of the book, they were happy accidents. ## observations

When a philosopher says that beauty consists of harmony, they mean that it consists of more harmony <i>than most people realise</i>. It is an observation that people are often surprised that a melody is not so beautiful when played without the accompaniment; or a bust is not so beautiful outside of a bracket, a diamond outside of its setting.

Also, looking across a range of paintings, the less-beautiful are noticeably less proportionate. There could also be too much proportion, on the other side, so it is a wedge, a post-it reminder that you should add more proportion. (Perhaps the mediaevals in fact did not have particularly harmonic art ; they are to the left of the continuum, nevertheless it could be a perennial observation that the amateurs are always a little to the left of the professionals.)

Philosophical observations on concepts (beauty, justice, truth, love) are partly trying to find a deeper pattern  - like finding that heat is motion of atoms - and partly correcting common applications of the word.

# 2012 / Development in Painting

**change in style of representation.**

- (a) here's a naive story: people have gradually learned to paint what is actually there, not what they think is there.  (Leonardo discovered hills should be blue and blurry; others discovered that objects should diminish with distance).
- (b) instead Gombrich says “making before matching” and “schema and correction”. For example Durer's Jerome in his study is a collection of tricks, to take into account different effects, rather than just a plainer way of copying nature.
- (c) Compare to building a house: post & girt, balloon frame, & then platform frame. The change is not in continuously better approximations to the ideal of a house.

- **parameters vs. functional forms.** Finding the right distinctions. choosing between models, each model has the same number of parameters, but different functional forms.

**when following inconsistent rules, the final product depends on starting point.**

**Some tricks:**

- (i) sunlight bounces back from your shoulder causing yellow limn to your jaw
- (ii) parts in shade are a little bluer, and highlights are little yellower, because the sun is yellow and the sky is blue. Impressionists exaggerate this, making the shadows differ from the highlights only by the hue, not the lightness.
- (iii) the four panes of a window reflecting separately.

**Some inconsistencies:**

- (i) inside / outside : the brightness of things outside is an order of magnitude higher, so the palettes shouldn't overlap
- (ii) naive paintings often have shade to show rounded body parts, but without shadow on the surface on which it is recumbent.
- (iii) when seated, facing the viewer, legs seem longer.
- (iv) buildings get flattened, so you can see both sides of an angle. Also when a wall is at an angle, the windows tend to be flatter.
