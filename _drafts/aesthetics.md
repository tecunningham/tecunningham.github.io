## Bad Art Exposes Ugly Things

**Bad art is actively bad.** Bad art is not the same as not-very-good art. Bad art is like a bad smell, bad breath, like a hug from someone you don't want to hug, it's something that you don't want to be in the same room as. It's not the same as art which is from different places, different times, which you can't tell whether it's good or not. Bad art is transparent, so you can see through it & see motives wriggling around -- needy, selfish, sentimental, jealous, mawkish. You see wish fulfillment - not made by someone who is an artist so they can make art, it is someone who makes art so they can be an artist. They keep painting while dishes pile in the sink and bills come through the letterbox.

I did a painting when I was 18 to show off that I had taken LSD.

## Painting & Iteration

**Painting & iteration.** We can recognize when a picture looks good or bad but at the same time can't say what would make it better or worse. The knowledge is implicit. This means that once you start painting you quickly see that there's something wrong with it, and when you try to fix it, it looks wrong in a different way, and you're in a loop.

The loop can end a few different ways. You accept some set of flaws and say it's finished. Or you keep circling, adding more brushstrokes, never admit defeat and never finish. Or you eventually deceive yourself into thinking that the flaws are gone -- and if other people think there are flaws then they are mistaken.

Because it's hard to anticipate whether something will look right it makes sense to just produce a lot and throw out the ones that don't work. People who don't throw out work may be people for whom the self-deception has set in.

It must be possible, eventually, to figure out how to smooth out all the bugs in a picture but it hardly seems realistic in a single lifetime -- perhaps if you stick to a very stylized set of techniques. Painters have been working for thousands of years and are still learning new things about representation.

Tolstory, in *Anna Karenina*: "Vronsky’s interest in painting and the Middle Ages did not last long. He had enough taste for painting to be unable to finish his picture. The picture came to a standstill. He was vaguely aware that its defects, inconspicuous at first, would be glaring if he were to go on with it."

Quentin Blake says somewhere in his autobiography that he would often have to throw out his best-looking drawings because they wouldn't be consistent with the style of the rest of the book, they were happy accidents. One day his publisher happened to see the drawings he was putting aside and told him to not worry about it - use them and never mind the inconsistency.

Christopher Dresser: "<i>Curves will be found to be beautiful just as they are subtle in character; those which are most subtle in character being most beautiful.</i>   The arc is the least beautiful of curves (I do not here speak of a circle, but of the line, as a line, which bound the circle); being struck from one centre its origin is instantly detected, while the mind requires that a line, the contemplation of which shall be pleasurable, must be in advance of its knowledge, and call into activity its powers of inquiry. The elliptic curve, or curve bounding the ellipse, is more beautiful than the arc, for its origin is not so strikingly apparent, being formed from two centres. The curve of the egg more beautiful still, being formed from three centres. As the number of centres necessary to the formation of a curve increases, the difficulty of detecting its origin also becomes greater, and the variety which the curve presents is also proportionally great; the variety being obviously greater as the number of the centres from which it is struck is increased."

<b>DEFINITIONS OF BEAUTY</b>

Pythagoreans (500BC) "Order and proportion are beautiful, and useful, while disorder and lack of proportion are ugly and useless"

Vitrivius (50BC): "proportion consists of the appropriate harmony arising out of the details of the work itself; the correspondence of each given detail among the separate details to the form of the design as a whole."

Galen (200): "Beauty does not consist in the elements, but in the harmonious proportion of the parts, the proportion of one finger to the other, of all the fingers to the rest of the and ... of all parts to all others, as it is written in the canon of Polyclitus."

Boethius (500): "we love similarity, but hate and resent dissimilarity."

Wiliam of Auxerre: "The goodness of a substance, and its beauty, are the same thing ... The beauty of a thing consists in these three attributes (species, number and order) - the very same in which Augustine says its goodness consists."

Alexander of Hales: "beauty is a disposition of the good in so far as it pleases the apprehension, whereas the good strictly speaking has to do with the disposition in which it pleases our affections."

Albertus Magnus (1200): "Just as corporeal beauty requires a due proportion of its members and splendid colours ... so it is the nature of universal beauty to demand that there be mutual proportions among all things and their elements and principles, and that they should be resplendent with the clarity of form."


<b>observations.</b>

When a philosopher says that beauty consists of harmony, they mean that it consists of more harmony <i>than most people realise</i>. It is an observation that people are often surprised that a melody is not so beautiful when played without the accompaniment; or a bust is not so beautiful outside of a bracket, a diamond outside of its setting.

Also, looking across a range of paintings, the less-beautiful are noticeably less proportionate. There could also be too much proportion, on the other side, so it is a wedge, a post-it reminder that you should add more proportion. (Perhaps the mediaevals in fact did not have particularly harmonic art ; they are to the left of the continuum, nevertheless it could be a perennial observation that the amateurs are always a little to the left of the professionals.)

Philosophical observations on concepts (beauty, justice, truth, love) are partly trying to find a deeper pattern  - like finding that heat is motion of atoms - and partly correcting common applications of the word.

(Note: we discover that both the perception of heat, and of beauty, depend on the context more than we realise. For the first, we say that our feeling is unreliable, context distorts it. For the latter, we say that context is part of beauty.)

(It is tautological to say "don't underestimate the weather", but it really means "the weather is worse than it may seem to you.")
