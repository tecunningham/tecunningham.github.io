# opposite sentences which both seem reasonable

* (A) He seemed like a nice guy because everyone told me he was a nice guy.
* (C) He seemed like a nice guy because everyone told me he was a real sleaze.

* (A) The correlation looks strong to her because she really believes in the effect.
* (C) The correlation looks weak to her because she really believes in the effect.

* (A) It seems hot in Canberra because everyone told me it would be hot.
* (C) It seems cool in Canberra because everyone told me it would be hot.

# setup of the problem

In perception there are a bunch of well-established effects in which your judgment of one sensation, $S_1$, is influenced by some other sensation, $S_2$.

For example colder things seem heavier; bluer things seem farther away; dark objects, when put next to light objects, seem darker.

# peoples' comments


* Carl: assimilation effects more likely with vaguer things -- like whether a person is a good guy -- than with more concrete perceptual things. Carl calls it "confirmation bias".
* Guy & John: if you have a strong prior, then seeing the church either pushes you up (to agree with the prior), or pushes you down (because you notice a strong disconfirmation of the prior). Calls it "availability".
* Mat: two different senses of "big" -- absolutely big, or bigger than expected -- & each sentence evokes a different one.
* Jacob: repetition sometimes emphasize sameness, sometimes highlights difference.
* Gharad: if I have something personal at stake, then perception will be biased towards expectations, otherwise normal perceptual effect of biasing away from expectations.
