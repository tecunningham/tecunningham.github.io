An attempt to think about how significant implicit preferences are. I'm ignoring the problem of how they can be measured, & just trying to think about how common they really are. I don't define implicit preferences, but I believe that all the cases here can be measured with a figure-8 intransitivity. I'm thinking about both conscious and unconscious implicit preferences.

Extra: can you explain *why* you made a choice, & whether you would've made it differently. Perhaps the biggest unconscious influence is that you think that *familiar* things are *sensible*.

[a common thread: judgment influenced by irrelevant things: different effect in direct than indirect. (examples)]

## Nutshell

1. **Fixation on particular issues leading to bias.** Some people get fixated on certain issues, causing their judgments to become distorted -- regarding politics, or race, or some particular fact, or their relationships with their colleague or their cousin or their spouse. However I believe that substantial biases of this type are not the norm.
2. **Justifying impulses.** I think it is common to practice minor self-deception in justifying your impulses to yourself: e.g. coming up with an excuse to smoke a cigarette, or to take another donut. However I'm not sure whether the presence of a justification makes a very big difference in actual behaviour -- i.e., if you can't find one excuse, you'll find another. Or you'll do it anyway. The justification might be just a layer of icing on the cake.
3. **Social norms.** It is common to modify your choices to obey social norms - e.g., you would take care to treat a male & female applicant in the same way if they both arrive at the same time - though you might treat them differently if they arrived separately. This will look like implicit preferences, though it can be perfectly conscious.
4. **Excess agreeableness.** I think it's common for your preferences to systematically change in the presence of other people - partly conscious, partly unconscious - and I think this probably has a large effect in making behaviour inconsistent.

## Race & Sex

Simple tests: we can look for the effect of race & sex on various measures: (1) grading exams, (2) hiring job candidates, (3) choosing tenants for an apartment, (4) sentencing convicted criminals, (5) setting welfare payments, (6) awarding scholarships. For each outcome we can measure explicit and implicit preferences separately. Much research finds a bias in this kind of setting although there is surely a strong selection effect in what is reported. E.g., Sendhil Mullainathan's NYT editorial lists a lot of findings, but he isn't listing the *average* findings in the literature, he's listing the most extreme findings, among those that are credible.

A digression regarding explicit discrimination: Insofar as it is based on beliefs, then we can make a distinction between (1) beliefs about demographic facts (about differences in education, crime, teenage pregnancy, IQ); (2) beliefs about the relationship of demographic facts and circumstances - i.e., beliefs about whether a change in circumstances would lead to a change in outcomes. I think that the latter is probably more important in the big picture: Lee Jussim says that in fact stereotypes are fairly accurate - people are fairly good judges of demographic facts. However it seems reasonable to say that, in most ages, people have underestimated the influence of circumstances on outcomes - people have observed differences in outcomes among groups and thought they were due to differences in essential character between those groups. Irish, Africans, Huns, Slavs, Chinese, English, women & men, fundamental characteristics to account for the differences in outcomes, and they keep underestimating the effect of circumstances (e.g. the debate between Carlyle and Mill).

There is an argument that discrimination is stitched into the fabric of everyday thought: (1) masculine default in language (“he”, “guys”, “human”, “man”); (2) difference in the words used - talk about black “thugs” and white “youths”; talk about a black “ghetto” and a white “poor neighborhood”; talk about black people “looting” and white people “scavenging.” There was a famous case of two similar photos with different captions, though by itself it is not conclusive. http://www.snopes.com/katrina/photos/looters.asp. (3) results of the IAT. I'm not convinced that these effects have a big effect for the majority of people.

4. Some people think about race or sex in partisan terms - becoming over-sensitive to any differences they can detect and dwelling on them, such that their judgments become distorted. I would guess this is important for a minority of people.

5. Finally I think there are strong social norms about equal treatment which will manifest as implicit preferences - i.e. people have certain conscious beliefs about race and sex, but they are uncomfortable expressing those beliefs directly in their behavior.  For example, when would you hold open the front door to your apartment, to let in a person without keys? I guess many people would (a) hold the door open for an old woman, (b) not hold it open for a young black man, but (c) hold it open either for both or for neither, if the woman and man were both waiting together.

6. Valuation of activities tainted by association. Work that is typically womans' work is thought to be low status, mindless. Music that is typically black is thought to be superficial - when the same thing is recorded by a white guy, people see the depth.

## Interpersonal behaviour.

When your decisions reveal some implicit considerations - being passive-aggressive, being fault-finding, or being perverse.

Examples:

1. A wife who always finds fault in her husband's decisions, or a husband finding fault in his wife's: if she turns left at a junction then the husband would say it was a bad decision, and if she turns right then he would've said the same. If the husband said "let's walk there," she'll say “what? in our best clothes?” if he said "let's take a taxi" she'll say “what? just around the corner?” Wife notices husband driving w one hand on the steering wheel, & says that it's unsafe, but in fact she does the same things when she's driving.
2. You blame your spouse for a bad decision when it turns out badly -- but you didn't blame them for the same decision when it turned out well (you only say it's a stupid thing to open the garage door with the manual release *after* you find out that doing that broke the door.).
3. If your pompous friend says he liked a movie, it makes you more likely to agree with a different friend who says they didn't like it, & vice versa.
2.  A teenager irritated by what their parent does - no matter what they do, it's irritating.
3.  A friend who contrives to be in the bathroom when the bill arrives.
4.  A colleague who, in meetings, agrees or disagrees with suggestions depending on who made the suggestion.
5.  Observing someone doing something unpleasant, and then deliberately working to spite them. If someone is standing very close behind me in a queue, I might sometimes let a gap open up in front of me, in part because I know it will annoy the person behind me. Seeing someone you dislike running towards you, you don't move out of their way, forcing them to slow down.

A general observation is that we all have certain areas of discretion: we can claim (a) to feel tired, (b) to feel sick, or (c) to have work to do. These can be used to cover implicit preferences. E.g.:

1. someone who is more likely to say they're busy with work when they are invited to dinner by someone they don't like;
2. a person who says they feel sick when they wants attention.

Overall I think everyone does this in small ways - making excuses, being perverse - but for only a minority of people does it substantially affect their decision-making.

## Status & power

Every little decision being angled so that it'll make you seem a bit smarter, a bit nicer.

Nietzsche says that all the things that Christianity glorifies are the things that are particular to the position of Jews around the time of Christ.

IMPULSES. Self-deception and casuistry with respect to impulsive things: cigarettes, alcohol, food, procrastination, sex, shopping.

I think most people have a contradictory internal dialogue when thinking about temptations - i.e., when we do something impulsive we don't just succumb, we usually find some justification. However this observation might not be very significant for understanding decision-making because people can almost always find some justification, so the presence or absence of any one justification may do little to affect behaviour. On the other hand, the struggle with temptation must be important: i.e., it is a large feature of many peoples' lives, their long battles with spending, drinking, eating, exercising, losing their temper; and people keep making resolutions that they break.

Examples:

1. Finding an excuse for behaviour in both X and not-X: e.g. drink in celebration of a win or consolation of a loss.
2. Cleaning your house when you have a lot of unpleasant paperwork to do.
3. Wouldn't choose alcoholic cider over non-alcoholic, but would choose $$\binom{\mbox{cider}}{\mbox{alcoholic}}\succ\mbox{water}\succ\binom{\mbox{cider}}{\mbox{non-alcoholic}}$$.

Overall: I can't come up with compelling cases where availability of an excuse to give in to an impulse affects behaviour in an important way.

## Consumption & aspiration

Change in preferences when observed, or a wish to have preferences that are more laudable.

Lots of classic studies find that your choice among goods (mugs, paintings, bottles of wine) is influenced by social markers: e.g. the price of the thing, or your knowledge that other people have said they like on of the things. These studies often don't rule out the possibility that people are inferring how valuable something is from how much other people like it. However there seems to be a common intuition that consumption is aspirational, beyond what can be justified by inference.

Behaving differently when observed:

1. When someone is watching your computer screen, you become more likely to read the NY Times and less likely to read Facebook.
2. When a friend accompanies you to the supermarket, you buy more vegetables and fewer bags of chips.
3. Scrubbing and flossing your teeth just before an appointment with the dentist.
4. Changing your preferences over jobs, or houses, or spouses, depending on who you're talking to, and your perception of what that person thinks.
5. Shyness about saying what you think: saying yes to a hairdresser, bartender, waiter, or salesperson, and later regretting that you didn't say no.

The French Marxist philosophers on consumption as status signaling (Baudrillard)

Overall: I think these effects are large - it matters a lot how people think of us, and how we think of ourselves.

## Partisanship

Shanto Iyengar at Stanford has a lot of research about size of party partisanship bias (& that it's bigger than feelings about social issues or race). The general phenomenon that all evidence comes to seem to support your prior hypothesis. I'm convinced that this is a common and systematic effect on judgment: people become significantly worse at evaluating facts once they start taking sides.

Note that the difference between a bias and just having strong priors is delicate. If you strongly believe that H is true then it can be rational for this to affect your evaluation of evidence, for example if you find a study which concludes not-H then the study is probably flawed. However a genuine partisan bias is logically distinct from this, and I think a well-designed experiment should be able to identify a real bias.

Examples:

1. scientists who interpret ambiguous tests favorably to their hypothesis (both X and not-X are interpreted as supporting H);
2. astrology, homeopathy, gene-behaviour correlations, priming studies - i.e., fields which have survived a long time with no true correlations;
3. Arsenal supporters thinking that referees are disproportionately mistaken when they give penalties to Arsenal;
4. democrats & republicans, saltwater & freshwater economists, catholics & protestants, Israelis & Palestinians.

Lots of examples from democrats & republicans:

1. Republicans claiming that fiscal stimulus is ineffective, but at the same time claiming that defence cuts will increase unemployment: http://www.nytimes.com/2011/10/31/opinion/bombs-bridges-and-jobs.html
2. the NY Times editorial page supporting the senate filibuster when it's in the Democrats interest for the filibuster to exist, and vice versa: http://www.themoneyillusion.com/?p=24959

Overall: I think that this is a common pathology. However perhaps it isn't very significant for economic decision-making because it's mostly just people talking about politics and intellectual things, with minor effects on their actual decisions.

## Framing
Extraneous influences when making a judgment.

– Examples: (1) When you're marking an exam script, and you need to make a judgment about the quality of the answer, all of the outside influences - the time of day, your mood, the handwriting, the prior exam. (2) When choosing among houses, influence of irrelevant things: the weather, the furniture, the estate agent, and your mood when you go to visit the house. (3) When choosing among investments, the way that the risk-return tradeoff is described can have a big influence on your judgment.

– Evidence: (1) a series of cute examples from Tversky & Kahneman. (2) evidence from the field on irrelevant influences - but there must be big selection effects in this literature.

– Overall: I think the general story is that judgment is very well calibrated in ordinary contexts, but can pick up irrelevant information when you are in an unusual context.

## Perception

In sensory perception I think there are very strong effects: your judgments about some feature of the world (distance, size, sound, color) are sensitive to irrelevant details. Unfortunately it's difficult to come up with clean examples because in most cases we do not have direct conscious access to the raw sensory data. Slate-Star-Codex has a recent [post](http://slatestarcodex.com/2016/08/25/devoodooifying-psychology/) about unconscious influences. How important are they really? He lists a bunch of findings in psychology that have been debunked recently, and says:

> What if people mostly make decisions based on reasonable factors ... and social assumptions are relatively powerless beyond a common sense level?

I think that unconscious influences *are* pretty important. I think that the psychologists were right, in the sense that they had the correct hunch. But they ended up fabricating their evidence because they found it so hard to quantify behaviour in the lab.

What's the prima facie case for unconscious influences?

1. Often I don't know why I do the things I do
2. Looking across the landscape of decisions, sometimes you can see them line up like iron filings, showing influences that the decision-makers are not aware of.

# (1) Difficulty Introspecting

I'm going to say that there are unconscious influences on my decisions if I can't accurately predict how I would act if the circumstances were different. That shows that I'm not conscious of how different factors contribute to my decisions.

This definition includes both cases where (A) I *think* I know what affects my decisions, and I'm wrong; or (B) I know that I don't know what affects my decisions.

We could then put the question a different way, instead of "how important are unconscious factors," say "how deep does conscious thought go?" Why would we assume that it goes all the way down? Animals make a lot of sensible decisions, but presumably don't have much insight into the interplay of reasons. We certainly have limited conscious insight into our *perceptual* judgments -- I couldn't explain what it is about my cousin that makes me recognize her, or what it is about the trajectory of a ball that makes me able to judge pretty accurately where it's going to land.

There are some shallow hypotheticals, which I think I can give roughly accurate answers to:

* Would I still run for the bus if I had 5 minutes, instead of 6?
* Would I buy this chicken if it were 6 dollars per pound, instead of 5 dollars?
* Would I have chosen to buy fish if the chicken wasn't available?

But there are also a lot of deeper ones, which I find pretty difficult to answer.

* Would I still run for the bus if I never saw other people running for the bus?
* Would I behave the same way towards colleague X if they were a woman instead of a man?
* What would I think of my parents if I was to meet them now, as strangers?
* What would I think of my work if I came across it as someone else's?
* Would my politics be the same if I had studied at a different school (where the professors' politics are different)?
* Would I feel the same way about my favorite album if it was sold in Starbucks?

(difference between shallow and deep: whether I often observe independent variation in those attributes)

# (2) Seeing Patterns in Decision-Making

1. **doing as others do.** A pretty big fact is that people tend to do & believe the things that people around them do & believe. A person's religious and political beliefs are correlated with their parents' religious & political beliefs. Beliefs about vaccines, about antibiotics, about whether to take off your shoes when you come inside, about the advisability of driving in a certain manner.

This doesn't imply unconscious influences. It would imply unconscious influences *if* people were not aware that their decisions are largely determined by who they are around. I.e., if they thought that they way they do things is the right way.

2. **evolutionary influences.** There are some influences on our decisions which, in retrospect, now we know them, are hard to deny. We feel tender about things which have big eyes (like babies); we enjoy sex even when it doesn’t lead to reproduction; we like the taste of sugar even when we know it’s bad for us; we get scared of snakes, spiders, of heights, of blood even when we see them behind glass - we are even scared of pictures of these things; we are disgusted by smells associated with infection, even when we know there’s no risk of infection.

# Conclusion

Let's circle back to the point that Scott Alexander was making. Recently a lot of studies, which purport to find unconscious influences on decision-making, have been debunked.

* **add:** Power posing might turn out to be bullshit; but for sure there are some magic things that make me persuaded by some people, and not persuaded by others. There's *something* there, but I guess it's deeper than just posing.
* **add:** There have been a lot of intellectual fads about unconscious influences: reinforcement learning; evolutionary psychology; Freudian psychology; postwar social psychology - people are conformists & rationalizers; .
* add: social psych argument: black kids do badly because of teachers expectations; big placebo effects; sex influences evaluation and performance influenced by expectations.
    - They are largely *right* in the big picture. There has been a massive win of nurture over nature, relative to position of 18th century. We say that nature determines half of variance, but that's within a fairly homogenous country.

A very influential paper (7000 citations!) arguing that unconscious influences are rampant: “Subjects are sometimes (a) unaware of the existence of a stimulus that importantly influenced a response, (b) unaware of the existence of the response, and (c) unaware that the stimulus has affected the response.”. A thorough critique of experiments that are intended to show important unconscious influences.

**Quine (1972) “Methodological reflections on current linguistic theory”**
When Quine wrote this, linguists were talking a lot about how people had unconscious knowledge of grammatical rules. Quine said that you can't say that someone knows a rule, from observing that their behaviour conforms to that rule, because there will always be infinitely many different rules which the behaviour satisfies. So this is Quine pouring some cold water on unconscious knowledge. However, in response, I say that we can still define unconscious knowledge underneath behaviour as the inability to accurately answer hypotheticals. So we don't need to specify *which* rule the person is using, but it's clear that they do not have conscious access to any rule which generates their observed behaviour.

There's an important distinction -

1. Cases when I don't know how I'd act if things were different.
2. Cases when I *think* I know, but I'm wrong.

Psychologists almost always talk about the second type of case -- where people are wrong about their motivations. But they should work more often on the first case. When I don't know how I'd act if things were different, it means I'm not conscious of my motivations. I'm doing what feels right, but I can't explain how my feeling depends on the situation, I can't answer counterfactual questions about what I'd do. I would guess that the first case is more common than the second case because it's pretty common for people to say "I don't know why I did that." And it means that there are large parts of our motives that we are not conscious of.

(Lots of argument over this: e.g. intuition vs deliberation -- whether intuition is *better* than deliberation.)

***

Finally here's a little observation about the history of unconscious influences. It's one of the few topics in philosophy that's relatively recent -- it wasn't until the early 19th century that people started talking about the ocean of the unconscious. This seems odd because if it was there all along, why did it take us so long to discover it? Perhaps this answer is too cute, but you could say that the unconscious is only a problem for people who expect to be able to give a reason for everything they do. Let's say that human motivation is an emulsion -- tiny drops of logic suspended in a gallon of instinct. We make little logical jumps, like preferring two dollars to one dollar, but most of the work is done by instinct, like when we have to choose between a kiss and a dollar, we ask our instincts, not our logic. Only in the 18th century did Europeans start to expect that we could construct an entire logical scaffolding around our decisions and give a reason for every choice. The experience of failing to do this led us to discover the unconscious.
