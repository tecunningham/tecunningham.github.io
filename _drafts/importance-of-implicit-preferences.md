An attempt to think about how significant implicit preferences are.

I'm ignoring the problem of how they can be measured, & just trying to think about how common they really are.

I don't define implicit preferences, but I believe that all the cases here can be measured with a figure-8 intransitivity.

I'm thinking about both conscious and unconscious implicit preferences.

[a common thread: judgment influenced by irrelevant things: different effect in direct than indirect. (examples)]

## NUTSHELL.

1. **Fixation on particular issues leading to bias.** Some people get fixated on certain issues, causing their judgments to become distorted -- e.g. regarding politics, or factual issues, or race, or relationship with family members or colleagues. However I believe that substantial biases of this type are not the norm.
2. **Justifying impulses.** I think it is common to practice minor self-deception in justifying your impulses to yourself: e.g. coming up with an excuse to smoke a cigarette, or another slice of cake. However I'm not sure whether the presence of a justification makes a very big difference in actual behaviour -- i.e., if you can't find one excuse, you'll find another. Or you'll do it anyway. The justification might be just a layer of icing on the cake.
3. **Social norms.** It is common to modify your choices to obey social norms - e.g., you would take care to treat a male & female applicant in the same way if they both arrive at the same time - though you might treat them differently if they arrived separately. This will look like implicit preferences, though it can be perfectly conscious.
4. **Excess agreeableness.** I think it's common for your preferences to systematically change in the presence of other people - partly conscious, partly unconscious - and I think this has a large effect.

## RACE & SEX.

1. Simple tests: we can look for the effect of race & sex on various measures: (1) grading exams, (2) hiring job candidates, (3) choosing tenants for an apartment, (4) sentencing convicted criminals, (5) setting welfare payments, (6) awarding scholarships. For each outcome we can measure explicit and implicit preferences separately. Much research finds a bias in this kind of setting although there is surely a strong selection effect.E.g., Mullainathan's NYT editorial lists a lot of findings, but I think he chose the most extreme findings he know of.

2. A digression regarding explicit discrimination: Insofar as it is based on beliefs, then we can make a distinction between (1) beliefs about demographic facts (about differences in education, crime, teenage pregnancy, IQ); (2) beliefs about the relationship of demographic facts and circumstances - i.e., beliefs about whether a change in circumstances would lead to a change in outcomes. I think that the latter is probably more important in the big picture: Lee Jussim says that in fact stereotypes are fairly accurate - people are good judges of demographic facts - but we also know that throughout history people have underestimated the influence of circumstances on outcomes - people have observed differences in outcomes among groups and ascribed them to differences in essential character between those groups.People have attributed to Irish, women, Africans, Huns, Slavs, Chinese, English, etc., fundamental characteristics to account for the differences in outcomes, and they keep underestimating the effect of circumstances (e.g. the debate between Carlyle and Mill).

3. There is an argument that discrimination is stitched into the fabric of everyday thought: (1) masculine default in language (“he”, “guys”, “human”, “man”); (2) difference in the words used - talk about black “thugs” versus white “youths”; talk about a black “ghetto” versus a white “poor neighborhood”; talk about black Katrina survivors “looting,” versus white survivors “finding.”There was a famous case of two similar photos with different captions, though by itself it is not conclusive. http://www.snopes.com/katrina/photos/looters.asp. (3) results of the IAT. I'm not convinced that these effects have a big effect for the majority of people.

4. Some people think about race or sex in partisan terms - becoming over-sensitive to any differences they can detect and dwelling on them, such that their judgments become distorted. I would guess this is important for a minority of people.

5. Finally I think there are strong social norms about equal treatment which will manifest as implicit preferences - i.e. people have certain conscious beliefs about race and sex, but they are uncomfortable expressing those beliefs directly in their behavior.A good example is holding open the front door to your apartment, to let in a person without keys: many people would (a) hold the door open for an old woman, (b) not hold it open for a young black man, but (c) if both came at the same time, then hold it open for either both or neither.

6. Valuation of activities tainted by association. Work that is typically womans' work is thought to be low status, mindless. Music that is typically black is thought to be superficial - when the same thing is recorded by a white guy, people see the depth.

## INTERPERSONAL. 
When your decisions reveal some implicit considerations - being passive-aggressive, being fault-finding, or being perverse.

– Examples: (1) A wife who always finds fault in her husband's decisions, or a husband finding fault in his wife's: if she turns left at a junction then the husband would say it was a bad decision, and if she turns right then he would say the same.Sally's sister & her husband: if he said let's walk from the station, she'll say “what? in our best clothes?” if he said let's take a taxi she'll say “what? just around the corner?”. Wife notices husband driving w one hand on the steering wheel, & says that it's unsafe, but she doesn't remember that she does the same herself. You blame your spouse for a bad decision when it turns out badly -- but you didn't blame them for the same decision when it turned out well (opening garage door with the manual release broke the opening mechanism, & you only say it's a stupid thing to do when you find that out). Jon's pompous friend: if the friend says he liked a movie, Jon more likely to agree w/ third friend who says they didn't like it, & vice versa. (2) A teenager irritated by what their parent does - no matter what they do, it's irritating. (3) A friend who contrives to be in the bathroom when the bill arrives. (4) A colleague who, in meetings, agrees or disagrees with suggestions depending on who made the suggestion. (5) Observing someone doing something unpleasant, and then deliberately working to spite them.E.g., if someone is standing too close behind me in a queue then sometimes I let a gap open up in front of me, knowing that it will annoy the person behind me. Seeing someone you dislike running towards you, you don't get out of their way, forcing them to slow down.

– A general observation is that we all have certain areas of discretion: we can claim (a) to feel tired, (b) to feel sick, or (c) to have work to do. These can be used to cover implicit preferences. E.g.: (1) someone who is more likely to say they're busy with work when they are invited to dinner by someone they don't like; (2) a person who says they feel sick when they wants attention.

– Overall: I think everyone does this in small ways - making excuses, being perverse - but for only a minority of people does it substantially affect their decision-making.

## STATUS & POWER.

– Every little decision being angled so that it'll make you seem a bit smarter, a bit nicer, .

– Nietzsche says that all the things that Christianity glorifies are the things that are particular to the position of Jews around the time of christ.

• IMPULSES. Self-deception and casuistry with respect to impulsive things: cigarettes, alcohol, food, procrastination, sex, shopping.

– I think most people have a contradictory internal dialogue when thinking about temptations - i.e., when we do something impulsive we don't just succumb, we usually find some justification. However this observation might not be very significant for understanding decision-making because people can almost always find some justification, so the presence or absence of any one justification may do little to affect behaviour. On the other hand, the struggle with temptation must be important: i.e., it is a large feature of many peoples' lives, their long battles with spending, drinking, eating, exercising, losing their temper; and people keep making resolutions that they break.

– Examples: (1) Finding an excuse for behaviour in both X and not-X: e.g. drink in celebration of a win or consolation of a loss. (2) Cleaning your house when you have a lot of unpleasant paperwork to do. (3) Wouldn't choose alcoholic cider over non-alcoholic, but would choose \binom{\mbox{cider}}{\mbox{alcoholic}}\succ\mbox{water}\succ\binom{\mbox{cider}}{\mbox{non-alcoholic}}
 .

– Overall: I can't come up with compelling cases where availability of an excuse to give in to an impulse affects behaviour in an important way.

## CONSUMPTION & ASPIRATION. 

Change in preferences when observed, or a wish to have preferences that are more laudable.

– Lots of classic studies find that your choice among goods (mugs, paintings, bottles of wine) is influenced by social markers: e.g. the price of the thing, or your knowledge that other people have said they like on of the things. These studies are often badly designed, because they don't rule out the possibility that people are simply inferring how valuable something is from how much other people like it. However there seems to be a common intuition that consumption is aspirational, beyond what can be justified by inference.

– The French Marxist philosophers on consumption as status signaling (Baudrillard)

– Behaving differently when observed: (1) When someone is watching your computer screen, you become more likely to read the NY Times and less likely to read Facebook. (2) When a friend accompanies you to the supermarket, you buy more vegetables and fewer bags of chips. (3) Thoroughly scrubbing and flossing your teeth just before an appointment with the dentist. (4) Changing your preferences about jobs, or spouses, or houses, depending on who you're talking to, and your perception of their preferences. (5) Shyness about saying what you think: saying yes to a hairdresser, bartender, waiter, or salesperson, and later regretting that you didn't say no.

– Overall: I think these effects are large - it matters a lot how people think of us, and how we think of ourselves.

• PARTISANSHIP.Shanto Iyengar at Stanford has a lot of research about size of party partisanship bias (& that it's bigger than feelings about social issues or race). The general phenomenon that all evidence comes to seem to support your prior hypothesis. I'm convinced that this is a common and systematic effect on judgment: people become significantly worse at evaluating facts once they start taking sides.

– Note that the difference between a bias and just having strong priors is delicate. If you strongly believe that H is true then it can be rational for this to affect your evaluation of evidence, for example if you find a study which concludes not-H then the study is probably flawed. However a genuine partisan bias is logically distinct from this, and well-designed experiment can identify a real bias.

– Examples: (1) scientists who interpret ambiguous tests favorably to their hypothesis (both X and not-X are interpreted as supporting H); (2) astrology, homeopathy, gene-behaviour correlations, priming studies - i.e., fields which have survived a long time with no true correlations; (3) Arsenal supporters thinking that referees are disproportionately mistaken when they give penalties to Arsenal; (4) democrats & republicans, saltwater & freshwater economists, catholics & protestants, Israelis & Palestinians.

– Lots of examples from democrats & republicans: (1) Republicans claiming that fiscal stimulus is ineffective, but at the same time claiming that defence cuts will increase unemployment;http://www.nytimes.com/2011/10/31/opinion/bombs-bridges-and-jobs.html (2) the NY Times editorial page supporting the senate filibuster when it's in the Democrats interest for the filibuster to exist, and vice versa.http://www.themoneyillusion.com/?p=24959

– Overall: I think that this is a common pathology. However perhaps it isn't very significant for economic decision-making because it's mostly just people talking about politics and intellectual things, with minor effects on their actual decisions.

## FRAMING. 
Extraneous influences when making a judgment.

– Examples: (1) When you're marking an exam script, and you need to make a judgment about the quality of the answer, all of the outside influences - the time of day, your mood, the handwriting, the prior exam. (2) When choosing among houses, influence of irrelevant things: the weather, the furniture, the estate agent, and your mood when you go to visit the house. (3) When choosing among investments, the way that the risk-return tradeoff is described can have a big influence on your judgment.

– Evidence: (1) a series of cute examples from Tversky & Kahneman. (2) evidence from the field on irrelevant influences - but there must be big selection effects in this literature.

– Overall: I think the general story is that judgment is very well calibrated in ordinary contexts, but can pick up irrelevant information when you are in an unusual context.

## PERCEPTION. 

In sensory perception I think there are very strong effects: your judgments about some feature of the world (distance, size, sound, color) are sensitive to irrelevant details. Unfortunately it's difficult to come up with clean examples because in most cases we do not have direct conscious access to the raw sensory data.
