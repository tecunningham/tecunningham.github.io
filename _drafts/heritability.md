[This site](http://match.ctglab.nl/#/multiple/twin_cor) compares correlations between identical and fraternal twins of various attributes.

These numbers are often interpreted as implying a split between three components: (1) genetic; (2) shared environment; (3) non-shared environment. 

Suppose two randomly selected people have 98% of genes in common, fraternal twins have 99% of genes in common, and identical twins have 100% of genes in common. (If there is assortative mating, then fraternal twins will have somewhat more). Then we extrapolate that.

1. **environmental amplification.** 
    - suppose that the people with the most ability do the most practice. E.g., the tallest kids practice basketball, so after a few years they are far better at it than average-height players, but if everyone had practiced then they would've all had similar ability.

2. **interactions.**
    - basic argument: suppose you need gene X and environment Y to both be present to produce Z. Then in the variance I think that decomposition *both* the genes and environment will be implicated, as seems reasonable.     - suppose probability p, and probability that both have it is q, and q=p^2 when independent.
    - then conditional probability is q/p, and correlation is (q/p-p)/(1-p).
