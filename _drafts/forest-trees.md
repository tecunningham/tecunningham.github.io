People are bad at judging things that they are familiar with. Some examples:

* I'll look at a drawing I did yesterday & realize "oh, the left arm is out of proportion"
* I'll read an article I wrote yesterday & realize "oh, the first sentence doesn't make any sense"
* I'm bad at judging whether my friends and family look old or young, pretty or ugly.

It seems paradoxical that we should be worse at judging things that we know better.

**Sniffer dog theory.**

A simple explanation is that our judgments are *encapsulated* - i.e. done by instinct - with little conscious insight - for example judging whether a drawing is well-proportioned; judging whether a sentence in grammatical; judging whether a person is male or female, gay or straight, korean or japanese.

Then why would familiarity distort our instinctive judgment?

A simple answer is that our judgment does not get worse - but we get worse at estimating the effect of information on *other people*. Suppose our pre-conscious system sends to the conscious brain the results of its inferences. As it gets more information about an object, it will send inferences based on more information, and the conscious brain will lose the initial inference, i.e. you'll lose the knowledge of how it appears at a first impression to other people.

Sniffer dog metaphor: in general, what your sniffer dog is interested in will be similar to what other sniffer dogs are interested in. However inside your own house, your dog is used to all the smells, so it could be hard to judge what other sniffer dogs are interested in.

In many cases we don't want to judge what something *is*, but want to judge how something *appears*. For example in judging a drawing, we only care about how it *appears*. Likewise with writing.

Can we think of a case where our judgment of what something *is* gets worse?
