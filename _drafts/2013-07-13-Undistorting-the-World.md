
# Undistorting the World

golden gate park, july 13 2013.


<p><i>That humans are all born with a bias: they fundamentally underestimate the humanity of other people; and history is endless stories of humans being surprised that other people are more like themselves than they expected. 
<br> And the implication is that we should indoctrinate children to be especially careful about people who are different from ourselves, something that is not natural but we must keep doing until it becomes an instinct; in the same way that you teach children to wash their hands.</i></p>

<ol>

 <li> In the late 19th century it was a cliche to say that the history of human understanding and civilisation was a history of the retreat of animism. That people are born as animists, interpreting everything as if it was conscious: a thunderstorm is punishment; the trees in the forest need to be placated; coincidences are trying to tell you something; ghosts of ancestors influence events in your life. Piece by piece we have learned that these patterns are made out of something different, something orthogonal to the world of people & intentions. The scientific world feels very different.

 <li> The opposite bias also exists. The bias in underestimating how deliberate others' decisions are; as if other people operated mechanically like a pebble rolling down a riverbed. This other bias remains in civilized people and is everywhere a cause of errors & suffering.

 <li> I.e., the tree is less conscious than it seems ; but also the tribe on the other side of the river is more conscious than it seems.

 <li> The bias manifests itself in people saying, "actually, now that i find myself in the their situation, doing it that way makes more sense than i'd realized." And this is said much more often than the opposite. I.e., people around the world systematically underestimate others' reasons.

 <li> Some small illustrations: travellers at first attribute the behaviour of foreigners to their disposition, but gradually discover it's the circumstances ; when people are exposed to a new type of food or a new type of music, overwhelmingly they end up appreciating it more than they expected to.

 <li> Some bigger illustrations: each nation, race and generation thinks of itself as unique, i.e. thinks of the others as not quite fully alive -- as not deeply suffering and experiencing joy in the same way. / This is not just that they <i>value</i> their own experience more -- rather they think that others' decisions and appreciations are less subtle, & so reading letters from another time or culture we are always surprised by the sensitivity.

 <li> Also we see it in the historical unfolding of egalitarianism: an aristocratic Englishman of the 18th century underestimated the sensitivity & reasonability of a peasant, of a woman, of a catholic, of an Irishman and an Ethiopian; he genuinely believed that they were all placed somewhere on the continuum between himself and the animal kingdom, and would be quite surprised to realise how rich their interior lives were. (probably each of these classes also underestimated all the others, though less extreme insofar as they shared in a culture which was aligned toward those who were in charge).

 <li> And contemporary societies have insular beliefs: Republicans and Democrats each believe that the other one lives morally and intellectually impoverished lives. 

 <li> A simpler thing: thinking you've cracked the code of dress, & wearing a fedora, is like thinking you've worked out the strategy of the guys playing 3-card monte, and are one step ahead. Thinking that everyone else is stupid.

</ol>

## other notes


- On animism: Charles Taylor, "disenchanted world", in his book "secular age" / Weber also talked about "disenchantment of the world". 

- examples: playing django reinhardt tunes / artists complaining / racists .

- counterpoint: people whose minds are so open their brains fall out ; who pretend to appreciate all others' cultures. / Also those who imitate too much, who think that the way others do it is .

- alternative: a solipsistic bias. From where you're sitting the branches of the trees form a pattern like a bird. There are certain repeating numbers in the scores of the football team you're following. / & you forget that the position from which you're seeing these things is arbitrary, there's no reason that the fundamentals should be arranged so that they produce a pattern from where you're sitting.

- Ross (1977): fundamental attribution error: when explaining others' behaviour, people systematically overestimate the influence of personality, & underestimate the influence of the situation.

- every person is always prepared for the scenery to fall away, the clouds to part, & it be revealed that the revolution of things is around themselves. 

- economists say *de gustibus non est disputandum*, & they see other social scientists always violating this -- i.e., other social scientists systematically underestimate the rationality and reasonableness of peoples' decisions, and with little justification they accuse people of making mistakes. / But the error of the social scientists may be an error of all humanity: underestimating others. 

- Kahneman, "Hawkish Biases" (2010): "we find, almost without exception, that the biases recently uncovered by  psychological research favor hawkish decisions in conflict situations."

- equilibrium in a world where we underestimate others: level-k thinking, cursed equilibrium, & herding: underestimating the strategy in others' decisions. (Herding is delicate: could go either way, either underestimating or overestimating others' information or sophistication). // Model: every agent thinks they have a finer partition (or more precise signal) than every other agent.
 
- George Eliot: *People are almost always better than their neighbors think they are.* (Against: no man is a hero to his valet).

- Allport, G. (1954), The nature of prejudice, New York, NY: Basic Books
   - Has the *contact hypothesis* -- that contact reduces prejudice.
